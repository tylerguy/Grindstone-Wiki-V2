Bourdicot Effect

The Bourdicot Effect is not named after a person but instead a textile which is in turn named after the place it developed.  The Bourdicot Effect is used to explain the partial characteristics of the Natural Laws within an overlay.  The Bourdicot Effect was proposed by Enchanter Iaen Garnier over a century ago and it is still used in consideration of the study of overlays now.  The Victorian Enchanter was originally interested in translocative magics but found determining more specific locations of translocation difficult without unintended side effects.  Those side effects and their severity became the starting point of their study and theory.

Simply put it is believed that during a planar overlay you are not uniquely in two different places at the same time but partially in one place or another.  To explain this within the ideas of 5-dimensional space caused a lot of frustration for early mathematicians (and eventually led to advancements in mathematics) that led to comfortable explanations for most phenomena being only expressions of real space.  While some theories have been proven to not be universally true under this comfortable math Garnier's explanation has persisted still as aforementioned.  This has been important to our current understanding of planes and how they interact in overlays.  According to the Bourdicot Effect planes occupy tiny spaces that almost randomly, or so it seems, to exist one place or another.  One plane might occupy 3-dimensional space for the observer in very tiny packets all over their body while experiencing another plane where that plane does not occupy.  The random placement in analogous to the Bourdicot textile something you might call Calico.

Garnier Packets

The larger the overlay the more evenly distributed the Garnier packets, the small spaces of one plane or another. In a perfect overlay, that is one plane is completely occupying space with another, the distribution would theoretically be 50% of each plane but data shows this is not exactly true and the number slightly fluctuates in time as the movement of planes are dynamic. The Bourdicot Effect lead to this discovery and another very important discovery. To displace between one plane and another you must experience an overlay first. By being present in a space of partial character between two planes you can then move into space of character higher than 50% than the native or originating plane for travel. This was the foundation to the restructuring of translocative spells and the utilization of nearby planes to displace within 3-dimensional space in one plane. In reality the translocated subject experiences a change in dominant plane and then rapidly the dominant plane becomes the native plane again. I'll provide a step by step process of this.

  1. A translocated subject experiences Garnier packets in a specified initial percentage of at least 11% non-native plane.
  2. The subject "moves" to space where the initial percentage climbs to an even distribution of native and non-native plane.
  3. With the move the subject becomes effectively off plane as they occupy space where the majority of Garnier packets experienced are non-native.
  4. In the predominantly non-native space a new space is "moved to" that may differ only slightly in dimension 4 and 5 but in the experienced 1, 2, and 3 they are far apart.
  5. The character of planes becomes even again.
  6. The character of native plane dominates and eventually the overlay ends as they separate.  This is a full translocation.

Planar Characteristics

The experienced character of a plane is dependent on the % overlay. This math changes with more planes and their relative sizes but the exact calculations are not what you might expect. For multi-planar interactions I will allow an expert to talk about them. They are extremely rare but sometimes forced for study.

When % character is mentioned it does not directly explain a phenomena or natural law experienced but it does lend to us studying the natural laws and aid to us making educated guesses to the changes.  If for instance a plane on the far edge of the first ring were to overlay with EXT-918 then time would seem to slow down as the character increases but since planes are dynamic the exact experience of time would vary.  Perhaps these two planes slight changes dynamically would not lend to extreme shifts in how time is experienced while there but if EXT-918 were to overlay with the second or third ring then the % character would have even more extreme effects on time as the slight shift by the dynamic overlay is experienced.
